The Client

The Kawartha Region Conservation Authority is a non-profit organization that was established under the Conservation Authorities Act in 1979 by its surrounding municipalities (Kawartha Lakes and County of Peterborough) consisting of about 2,563 square kilometres. Kawartha Conservation is responsible for managing and monitoring the natural resources that play a direct role in ensuring water quality and quantity within the area for both environmental and human needs. They achieve this through watershed planning, stewardship, monitoring, research, and management (About us, Kawartha Conservation, 2022).

Problem Statement

Kawartha Conservation identified that lake stewards and stakeholders would benefit from having a tool that presents water quality parameters such as temperature and dissolved oxygen in an easy to use and quickly understandable format. Our tool will hopefully increase public engagement and awareness of the lake's overall lake health. A heightened understanding of Lake Simcoe benefits all who use the lake and the creatures who call this lake home. Our Process

Our Process

Our main goal for the project was to visually render two key factors for lake water quality in an accessible format: dissolved oxygen and water temperature. We narrowed our scope to 2019 provincial data from six monitoring stations across the lake. A key feature that we needed for this time-series data was a user-interactive time-slider.

Initially, we approached building our solution by leveraging out-of-the box tools, such as Experience Builder, but quickly found that our need to display time-series data required customizations that were not possible in that environment. As a hybrid solution, we chose to build with Esri's JavaScript API widgets in conjunction with Github. This afforded us the flexibility and customization we needed to render the time-slider feature.

Once the time-slider feature was established, we added in two web layers, one for dissolved oxygen and water temperature, respectively. This data was processed in ArcGIS Pro and then added to ArcGIS Online, whereby it was brought into our JavaScript API webpage. We then needed a way for the user to select which layer to display. We experimented with different ways of having the user select data layers. In the interest of UX and compact design, we opted to have an eye icon within the legend to toggle each layer on or off. For fun and to broaden the use of the visualized data, we included a print option for the user. This is discretely nested on-screen as an expandable printer icon.

Throughout our development process, we sought a web solution that would be easily adapted for use with other lakes and their datasets. Given more time, we would be interested in bringing bathymetry to the visualization and provide the user context for the relationship between dissolved oxygen, water temperature, and how that affects fish habitat quality.
